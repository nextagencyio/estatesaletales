I've been running estate sales in the Mesquite area for a long time, and I thought I'd seen everything. Hoarder houses, family fights over Grandma's china, people trying to haggle the price on a fifty-cent paperback. But nothing -- and I mean nothing -- prepared me for Mr. Henderson.

Mr. Henderson was in his late eighties. Sharp mind, sweet disposition, and absolutely no interest in wearing clothes.

Here's how it was supposed to work. Mr. Henderson was still living in the house. This happens sometimes -- the family is downsizing Dad's belongings while he's still there, maybe clearing out the extra rooms before a move to a smaller place or assisted living. It's not unusual. You work around it. The family sets up a comfortable room for the homeowner, stocks it with snacks and a TV, and everybody agrees that during sale hours, Dad stays put.

Mr. Henderson's family set him up in the master bedroom. Nice TV, recliner, mini fridge with his favorite drinks. The door would stay closed. We put a sign on it: "Private -- Not Part of Sale." Simple. Clean. Foolproof.

It was not foolproof.

Saturday morning, the sale was humming. We had a good crowd, maybe thirty or forty people browsing through the house at any given time. The kitchen was packed -- kitchenware always draws a crowd, and Mrs. Henderson had accumulated an impressive collection of cast iron and vintage Pyrex that people were fighting over.

I was in the living room helping a lady carry a floor lamp to the checkout table when I heard a sound that will live in my memory forever. It was a gasp. Not one gasp -- a collective gasp. The kind of sound a crowd makes when something happens that nobody expected.

I turned toward the kitchen.

There was Mr. Henderson. Standing in the kitchen doorway. Buck naked.

Not a stitch. Not a robe, not boxers, not even socks. Just Mr. Henderson, in all his glory, blinking at a room full of strangers who were holding his wife's Pyrex bowls.

"Could someone get me a glass of water?" he said. Calm as could be. Like this was perfectly normal.

The kitchen went dead silent. Forty people frozen mid-browse. A woman near the stove was holding a cast iron skillet and looked like she might use it as a shield. A man by the refrigerator was staring at the ceiling like his life depended on it.

I moved fast. Grabbed a dish towel -- the biggest one I could find, which was not big enough -- and walked over to Mr. Henderson.

"Hey there, Mr. Henderson. Let's get you that water and get you back to your room, okay?"

"Oh, is there a sale going on?" he asked, genuinely surprised. "I forgot."

He'd forgotten. The man had simply forgotten that fifty strangers were in his house shopping through his belongings, and he'd walked out of his room the way he apparently always walked around his house -- wearing nothing at all.

I wrapped the dish towel around his shoulders -- again, it did not cover what needed covering -- and walked him back to the bedroom. Got him his water. Made sure the TV was on. Closed the door. Then I went back to the kitchen, where the entire room was still standing in stunned silence.

"Well," I said, "that's something you don't see at a garage sale."

That broke the tension. People laughed. The shopping resumed. The cast iron lady bought six pieces. The man who'd been studying the ceiling bought a set of mixing bowls and left without making eye contact with anyone.

Mr. Henderson came out two more times that day. Both times, I'd posted one of my helpers near his door, but the man was surprisingly quick for his age. The second time, he'd managed to put on a hat. Just a hat. Nothing else. Just a Dallas Cowboys cap, like that was the missing piece of the outfit.

By Sunday, the family had arranged for Mr. Henderson to spend sale hours at his daughter's house. He went happily. I don't think he ever quite understood what the fuss was about.

I tell this story at every estate sale conference, every meetup, every time a new liquidator asks me what the job is really like. Because this is what the job is really like. You can plan everything perfectly -- the pricing, the staging, the advertising, the crowd control -- and a naked octogenarian in a Cowboys hat will still find a way to be the most memorable part of your weekend.

God bless Mr. Henderson. I hope he's somewhere comfortable, walking around his house exactly the way he likes.
