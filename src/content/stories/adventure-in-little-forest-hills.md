One of the last estate sales my friend Martha did — and maybe why she decided to retire from the estate sale business — was for a friend in an area of town known as Little Forest Hills. Close by are grand homes and the world-renowned Dallas Arboretum and Botanical Gardens, but the vibe in the Little Forest Hills residential area is sort of "funky chicken."

When friends help friends do an estate sale, there really should be some kind of disclosure clause in the initial conversation about known "nut jobs" who live in the neighborhood. The tried-and-true expression "forewarned is forearmed" would have been helpful here. In defense of Martha's friend Jerri, though, she just assumed that Sandra from down the street would still be court-ordered to "stay on her meds" after the most recent event where police and fire rescue came to Sandra's house. When they pulled her out of her attic, naked with her head shaved, she was taken away for evaluation and given a prescription or two. Too bad she didn't stay confined long enough to miss the estate sale down the street.

But on the first morning of the sale, Sandra arrived bright and early in her fuzzy slippers and robe, wanting to be helpful. "Anyone need coffee and Bailey's or maybe a shot of tequila to get your motor running?" "No, Sandra, but thanks for coming over in the dark to knock on the door. We open at 9:00 a.m., you know, when it's light outside."

And back she was, first in line. She bought a dresser with a mirror, a trunk, a dining table with six chairs, a wagon, a rocking chair, plus piles of miscellaneous things which she swore she didn't need but were just too cute to pass up. Since she lived just two doors down, Jerri said it was fine if she took a couple of things home and came back to pay later. But "Remember, Sandra, cash only!" Truth be told, estate liquidators can overlook a lot of crazy in someone spending a lot of money.

Off she went with the dresser and mirror, scooting it on the pavement all the way to her house. Words can't describe the sound. Only a miracle kept it from turning into splinters before she got it home. She returned several times and scooted more to her house, all the while her merchandise in her front yard was beginning to take on a life of its own. Then Sandra came back to pay. She had been saving the quarters that her grandmother left her in her will, and the little wagon she bought at the sale was perfect for dragging them to make her payment of $575.00 in UNROLLED quarters. After counting and recounting, and with maybe some soft cursing under her breath, Martha found the avid shopper was still $169 short. Sandra promised to return soon with the extra amount owed, along with a promise to do more shopping.

While Sandra was loading more quarters into her little red wagon, she had a few more drinks, and now her shopping skills were honed. For this last round of impulse buys, she called to say she would need to return with a check to add to the wagon full of quarters. Martha only agreed to that because it was getting too late in the day to count another $300 in small change.

After a couple of hours, there was still no sign of Sandra. Martha walked down to her house, rang the bell, and knocked on her door. Dogs were barking their heads off, but there was no sign of Sandra. Martha walked up the driveway, and through the locked gate, she could see the lifeless-looking body of the estate sale's most prolific shopper. She was stretched out in a lawn chair, mouth open, seriously looking dead.

In a panic, Martha yelled at her, banged on the fence, drove the dogs wild, and yet no response. Not even a stir. So, she did what any concerned citizen would have done, she called the Police and Fire Rescue. They came. They recognized the address from the previous naked-in-the-attic removal from a few weeks before. She was dead...dead drunk.

Needless to say, when she came back to life, Sandra was livid that the cops had been called. Martha had poked the bear! What followed was not a pretty confrontation between Sandra and Martha. But in the end, Martha got her money, and the police randomly patrolled the street 'til the sale was over.
