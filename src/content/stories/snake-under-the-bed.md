We'd been in the house for maybe ten minutes when I heard a scream from the back bedroom. Not a "found a spider" scream. A real one. The kind that makes you drop your clipboard and run.

There was Linda, standing on the bed, pointing at the floor. And there it was -- a four-foot black snake, coiled up between the nightstand and the wall, looking at us like *we* were the intruders.

Which, to be fair, we were.

This was a Wednesday afternoon in North Dallas, two days before the sale. We were doing what we always do -- walking the house, tagging furniture, opening drawers, making sure nothing valuable gets overlooked and nothing embarrassing gets displayed. Standard prep. The family had told us everything was cleared out, the homeowner had moved into assisted living months ago, and we had the place to ourselves.

Nobody mentioned a roommate.

Linda climbed off the bed very carefully, and we both backed out of the room like we were in a hostage negotiation. I shut the door and stuffed a towel under it. Then I did what any professional estate sale operator would do: I called my husband.

"There's a snake in the house."

"What kind?"

"The kind I don't want to be near."

He wasn't helpful.

I called animal control next. They told me they could send someone out, but it might be a day or two. A day or two. We had a sale starting Friday morning. I had price tags to put on everything in that bedroom, including the bed the snake had claimed as its own personal territory.

So I called a pest control company. God bless them, they came out that same afternoon. The guy walked in with a pair of tongs and a five-gallon bucket like this was just another Tuesday for him. Which, apparently, it was.

"Texas rat snake," he said, lifting it out from behind the nightstand like he was picking up a garden hose. "Harmless. Probably been living here awhile. They like old houses."

The snake went into the bucket. The bucket went into his truck. And I went back to pricing lamps with shaking hands.

Here's the part that still gets me. We ran that sale Friday, Saturday, and Sunday. Nearly two thousand people walked through that house over three days. Two thousand people who browsed through that back bedroom, opened the nightstand drawers, sat on the bed to test the mattress. Two thousand people who had no idea they were shopping in a snake's living room.

I thought about putting up a sign. Something like "Recently de-snaked -- shop with confidence." Linda talked me out of it.

The thing about estate sales is that you never really know what you're walking into. The families give you the rundown -- "Mom collected Hummels, Dad had tools in the garage, there's some nice furniture upstairs" -- but they never mention the things that actually make your day interesting. Nobody ever says, "Oh, and there might be wildlife."

After twenty-plus years of running Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales in the DFW area, I've found all kinds of creatures in all kinds of places. Raccoons in attics. Possums in garages. A family of squirrels that had set up shop inside a grandfather clock. But that snake in the North Dallas bedroom holds a special place in my memory, because it was the first time I truly understood that this job would never, ever be boring.

Linda still brings it up at least once a month. Usually when we're walking into a new house for the first time.

"You check under the beds," she says. "I'll start in the kitchen."

I always check under the beds now. Every single time.
