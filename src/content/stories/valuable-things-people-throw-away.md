The call usually starts the same way. A family member, often a daughter or son, slightly overwhelmed, slightly apologetic: "I don't think there's anything valuable in the house. It's mostly just... stuff."

After twenty-plus years of running Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales in the Dallas-Fort Worth area, I have learned to never, ever take that at face value. Because the families are almost always wrong. Not about the "mostly stuff" part -- that's usually true. But about the "nothing valuable" part? That's where it gets interesting.

Some of our biggest discoveries have come from houses where the family was ready to call a junk hauler and be done with it.

## The Painting Behind the Door

A house in University Park, maybe fifteen years ago. The family had already done their walk-through and taken everything they wanted. What was left, they told us, was "just furniture and junk." Fine. We started prepping.

In the back bedroom, behind the door, leaning against the wall and half-covered by a bathrobe hanging from a hook, was a painting. Not framed. No signature visible from the front. About two feet by three feet. My partner Linda picked it up, looked at the back, and said, "Susan, there's a name on this."

I won't say whose name, because the family has asked me not to. But I will say that we called an art appraiser that afternoon, and the painting turned out to be an original work by a well-known mid-century Texas artist. The family had walked right past it. The junk hauler would have tossed it. It sold at auction for over forty thousand dollars.

The daughter called me afterward. "It was behind the *door*," she kept saying, like she still couldn't believe it. "Behind the door."

## The Jewelry in the Coat Pocket

This one happens more than you'd think, which is why I always check pockets. Always. Every coat, every jacket, every pair of pants in the closet.

At a sale in Richardson, I was going through a hall closet full of men's winter coats. Nice coats, but nothing special. In the inside breast pocket of a wool overcoat, I found a small velvet pouch. Inside the pouch was a diamond ring. Not a modest diamond ring. A "someone spent a lot of money on this" diamond ring.

I called the family immediately. Turns out it was the wife's engagement ring -- the original one, from 1958. She'd lost it decades ago and they'd assumed it was gone forever. The husband had apparently found it at some point, tucked it in his coat pocket meaning to surprise her, and then forgotten about it. He'd passed away three years before the sale. She'd been wearing the replacement ring ever since.

She cried when I gave it back to her. I cried too. Her daughter cried. We all just stood there in the hallway crying over a coat pocket.

## The First Edition in the Box of Paperbacks

Books are tricky. Most of the time, a box of old books is worth exactly what it looks like -- a box of old books. Paperback westerns, romance novels, outdated encyclopedias. The donation pile is full of them.

But once in a while, you find something. At a sale in Fort Worth, I was going through a box of books that the family had already labeled "DONATE." Just tossed in there with no organization. Dime-store mysteries, a few cookbooks, some Reader's Digest condensed volumes. And sitting right there in the middle, spine cracked and cover faded, was a first edition of *To Kill a Mockingbird*.

Not a book club edition. Not a later printing. A true first edition, first printing, 1960. With the dust jacket, which was rough but intact.

I about fell over.

We had it authenticated and it sold to a rare book dealer for a number that I promised the family I wouldn't share publicly. But I will say it was more than the entire rest of the estate sale combined. From a box marked "DONATE."

I tell every family now: let us look through the books before you donate them. Please. Just let us look.

## The "Junk" Ceramics

A family in Plano was clearing out their grandmother's house. She'd been a hobbyist potter, they said. Made little bowls and vases in her garage studio. "Cute stuff, but nobody's going to want Grandma's pottery."

Grandma's pottery, it turned out, included about a dozen pieces that were not her own work. Mixed in with her handmade bowls were pieces she'd collected over the years from other artists -- including several pieces of signed studio pottery from well-known mid-century ceramicists. She'd picked them up at flea markets and estate sales herself, recognizing quality that the sellers had missed.

The irony was almost too perfect. A woman who'd spent her life finding undervalued pottery nearly had her own collection undervalued by her own family. We brought in a ceramics appraiser, and several pieces sold for hundreds of dollars each. One piece -- a signed vase that had been sitting on a shelf in the garage holding paintbrushes -- sold for over two thousand.

## The Vintage Guitars in the Attic

"There might be some old guitars up in the attic," the son told us. "Dad played a little when he was young. They're probably junk."

There were four guitars in that attic in Irving. Two were, in fact, junk -- beat-up acoustics with warped necks and missing strings. The other two were a 1959 Gibson Les Paul and a 1962 Fender Stratocaster, both in their original cases, both in remarkable condition considering they'd been sitting in a Texas attic for decades.

I don't know a lot about guitars, but I know enough to know that certain vintage models are worth serious money. I called a friend who deals in vintage instruments. He came over, opened the cases, and his hands were shaking.

Combined, those two guitars were worth more than the house.

The son sat down on the attic steps and stared at the wall for a while. "He just played them in the garage on weekends," he said. "He had no idea."

## The Lesson

Here's what I've learned after two decades of finding valuable things in houses where "there's nothing valuable": people don't always know what they have. And that's not a criticism -- it's just human nature. We live with our things every day, and after a while, they become invisible. That painting is just the painting behind the door. That ring is just something lost years ago. Those guitars are just Dad's old hobby.

That's why estate sale professionals exist. Not just to price things and run a sale, but to look -- really look -- at everything in a house before anything gets donated, tossed, or hauled away.

Every time a family tells me there's nothing valuable, I smile and say, "Let's take a look anyway." And about one time in five, we find something that changes the whole picture.

Check the coat pockets. Look behind the doors. Open the boxes marked "DONATE."

You never know.
