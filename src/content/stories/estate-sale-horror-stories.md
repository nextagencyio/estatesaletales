People always ask about the best estate sales. The big finds, the record crowds, the houses with incredible stuff. And sure, I love telling those stories. But the stories that really bond estate sale professionals together -- the ones we trade at conferences over drinks, the ones that make us laugh until we cry -- are the disaster stories. The days when everything went sideways and we had to figure it out on the fly.

After twenty-plus years of running Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales in the DFW area, I have a collection of these. A big collection. Here are the ones that still make my team groan when I bring them up.

## The Wrong House

I'm putting this one first because it's the one I'll never live down.

Early in my career, we were hired to do a sale for a family in Garland. Nice neighborhood, older homes, lots of similar-looking houses. I was given the address over the phone. I wrote it down. Or at least I thought I did.

We showed up on Wednesday to start prepping. The door was unlocked -- the family had told us they'd leave it open. We walked in and started working. Measuring rooms, planning the layout, making notes on the furniture. We were there for about two hours before the front door opened and a woman walked in carrying groceries.

She was not the homeowner who'd hired us. She was the homeowner of the house next door. The actual house. The one we were supposed to be in. Because I had transposed two numbers in the address, we had spent two hours prepping a stranger's home.

The woman was remarkably calm, all things considered. "I was wondering why my door was unlocked," she said.

We apologized profusely. We had not moved or touched anything -- we were still in the assessment phase, thank God. But the sheer embarrassment of explaining to a stranger that you've been walking through her house with a clipboard and pricing gun, evaluating her belongings for a sale she didn't authorize, is something I hope to never repeat.

I now confirm the address three times. I verify it with the family. I check the mailbox. I once checked the name on a package on the porch. I am never, ever going into the wrong house again.

## The Pipe That Burst

A January sale in a house in Plano. Texas doesn't do winter well, as anyone who's lived here knows. The house had been vacant for about a month while the family got the estate ready. The heat had been left on, but someone -- and we never figured out who -- turned it off during a cold snap.

We arrived Friday morning to open the sale and found the front hallway under two inches of water. A pipe in the upstairs bathroom had frozen and burst overnight. Water was cascading down the stairwell like a decorative fountain that nobody had ordered.

We had forty people lined up outside. The sale was advertised. People had driven from across the metroplex.

We sent everyone home. Well, we tried. Some of them wanted to shop anyway. "I don't mind getting my feet wet," one man said, reaching for the door handle. Sir, the house is flooding. "I just need the china cabinet. Is the china cabinet wet?"

We spent the rest of the day with a water extraction company instead of running a sale. The sale was rescheduled for the following weekend. The china cabinet survived. The man came back for it.

## The Family Argument

I have a rule: family disputes get resolved before the sale, not during. I insist on it. Every family member signs off on the arrangement. Every family member agrees on what's being sold and what's being kept. We handle this upfront because I learned -- the hard way -- what happens when you don't.

A sale in Irving, maybe ten years ago. Two sisters, one brother. Parents had both passed. The house was being sold, the estate divided, everything was supposed to be settled. We opened on Friday morning.

By Friday afternoon, one of the sisters showed up with a moving truck. She'd decided that she actually wanted the dining room set that was currently being sold. She walked in, told a shopper to put down the candlesticks, and started wrapping the centerpiece in newspaper.

Her sister showed up twenty minutes later. Words were exchanged. Loud words. In the driveway. In front of shoppers. The brother called me, yelling, from somewhere in Oklahoma.

I had to shut down the sale for an hour while the family sorted things out on the front lawn. Shoppers were watching from their cars like it was a drive-in movie. One of them brought a lawn chair from the garage and sat on the sidewalk.

The family worked it out -- the dining set was split between the sisters in a way that satisfied no one, which I've learned is actually the sign of a fair compromise. The sale resumed. The lawn chair man bought a set of golf clubs.

## The Tornado Warning

You cannot control the weather. This is a thing I tell myself often, usually while standing in a house full of merchandise and watching the sky turn green.

We were running a sale in Mansfield during spring storm season. Saturday morning started beautiful. By early afternoon, the sky went dark and the tornado sirens went off.

In North Texas, you learn to take the sirens seriously. We herded about thirty shoppers into the interior hallway and the master bathroom. Thirty strangers, crammed into a hallway, surrounded by estate sale merchandise, listening to the wind howl outside and the weather radio crackle warnings.

One woman continued shopping from the hallway. She found a set of vintage Tupperware on a shelf next to the bathroom and asked if I could hold it for her until the tornado passed.

The tornado didn't hit us. It touched down about four miles south. When the all-clear came, everyone filed back out and resumed shopping like nothing had happened. The Tupperware lady bought her Tupperware and three other things from the hallway.

Texans are a different breed.

## The Electricity Went Out

A summer sale in a house with no backup generator and no natural light. The house was one of those 1970s builds with small windows and dark wood paneling. When the power was on, the lamps made it charming. When the power went out at 10 AM on Saturday -- a transformer blew down the street -- it was a cave.

We had a house full of shoppers. Nobody could see anything. People were using their phone flashlights to browse, which would have been fine except that one gentleman tripped over an ottoman in the dark and knocked over a shelf of glassware.

We cleaned it up by flashlight. We opened all the curtains and propped open the doors for natural light. We borrowed a generator from a neighbor -- bless that man -- and got enough power for a few lamps and the credit card reader. The sale went on.

The gentleman who knocked over the glassware felt terrible. He bought everything that wasn't broken, which was most of it. He also bought the ottoman.

## The Takeaway

Every single one of these stories ended fine. That's the thing about estate sale disasters -- they're only disasters in the moment. An hour later, a day later, a week later, they're just stories. Good ones. The kind you tell at dinner parties.

The wrong house taught me to triple-check everything. The burst pipe taught me to confirm the utilities. The family argument taught me to get agreements in writing. The tornado taught me that Texas shoppers will browse through the apocalypse.

And the power outage taught me to always, always carry a flashlight.

Twenty years of everything going wrong, and I'd do every single day of it again.
