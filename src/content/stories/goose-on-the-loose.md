There are things they don't cover in any business course. How to handle irate customers, sure. How to manage cash flow, absolutely. But nobody -- and I mean nobody -- prepares you for the moment a full-grown Canadian goose decides to set up camp in the middle of a busy street during your estate sale.

It was a Saturday morning in Dallas, peak sale hours. We were running a big one in a house just off Mockingbird Lane, which, if you know Dallas at all, you know is not the kind of street where you want anything blocking traffic. It's four lanes of people who are already annoyed and running late. Adding a large, angry bird to the situation was not ideal.

I was inside handling the checkout line when one of my helpers came running in. "Susan, there's a goose."

"A what?"

"A goose. In the street. A big one."

I walked outside, and sure enough, there it was. A Canadian goose the size of a golden retriever, standing in the right lane of Mockingbird like it had an appointment. Cars were swerving around it. Someone honked. The goose honked back. The goose was winning.

My first instinct was to call someone. That's what reasonable people do. I called animal control. I explained the situation. There's a goose. It's in the road. It's going to cause an accident or get hit. Can you send someone?

"Ma'am, we don't handle geese."

I'm sorry, what?

"Geese are federally protected migratory birds. We can't remove them. You could try calling Parks and Wildlife."

I called Parks and Wildlife. They were very sympathetic. They also couldn't help. They suggested I "give it space and wait for it to move on its own."

Meanwhile, traffic on Mockingbird was backing up. My shoppers were having trouble getting to the sale. The goose was honking at a Buick. Things were escalating.

I am not a wildlife expert. I have no training in waterfowl management. But I am a woman who has run estate sales in the Dallas-Fort Worth area for over twenty years, and if there's one thing this job has taught me, it's that when nobody else is going to handle the problem, you put on your big-girl boots and handle it yourself.

I went back inside, grabbed a large moving blanket from the garage, and walked out into the street.

The goose saw me coming. It spread its wings, which I swear spanned about six feet, and made a sound that I can only describe as "prehistoric." Every instinct in my body said to turn around. Instead, I did what I always do when something terrifying is happening: I talked to it.

"Hey there, buddy. Hey. You can't be here. You're blocking traffic and you're scaring my customers and honestly, you're scaring me too, but we're going to figure this out."

The goose hissed. Canadian geese hiss. I did not know this before that morning. I know it now, and I will never forget it.

I held the blanket out wide, making myself look bigger -- something I vaguely remembered from a nature show -- and slowly walked toward the goose, sort of herding it toward the side of the road. It took a step back. Then another. Then it decided I was bluffing and charged at me.

I may have screamed. I may have also said some words I wouldn't repeat at church. But I held my ground, mostly because my feet were frozen in place from terror. The goose stopped about three feet away, gave me one last demonic hiss, then turned around and waddled -- with incredible dignity -- across the remaining lanes and onto the grass on the far side of the road.

I walked back to the house to applause from a small group of shoppers who had been watching from the yard.

"You should start a wildlife removal company," one of them said.

"I should start charging extra," I said.

The goose hung around the neighborhood for the rest of the weekend. We'd spot it in different yards, on different lawns, always looking slightly offended by everything around it. By Monday it was gone. Migrated on to ruin someone else's Saturday, I assume.

I still drive down Mockingbird Lane sometimes and think about that goose. Somewhere out there, it's probably standing in another road, honking at another Buick, completely unbothered. I respect it, honestly. That goose knew exactly what it wanted, and it wasn't going to let a moving blanket and a panicked estate sale lady change its mind.
