A couple of years ago, we did a sale for a lovely lady who lived on a narrow street in Garland, Texas. Estate sale parking is always at a premium. On this particular street, cars could only park on one side of the street or the other, but not both at the same time.

Cars entering or exiting the street had to zigzag among the parked cars. In these situations, it's usually a game of "chicken" to see whether a car coming toward you will pull over anywhere they possibly can, so that you can either find a place to park or get down the street past the parking mayhem.

To make things more complicated at this particular sale, the houses on this street all had curbside mailboxes. The mailman on a normal day could pull their truck up close to the curb and do the incoming and outgoing mail exchange inside the mailbox, just by reaching through their driver's side window.

Mailmen HATE estate sales. Our customer traffic is in their way big-time! You can only imagine the aggravation we cause UPS, FedEx, Amazon, and sanitation truck drivers. At any given point in the sale, we could have 50 cars, pick-ups, and SUVs lining the street while their drivers are inside the sale seeking treasure. And it's not unusual for some shoppers to stay inside "oohing and aahhing," poking around for an hour or so. It's my understanding that if a mailbox is blocked by a car so that the mail carrier can't drive close to your mailbox, he or she is not obligated to deliver the mail that day.

Now, most of the mailmen will go beyond the call of duty, park in the middle of the street for a minute — if the middle of the street is available — and quickly deliver the mail. The UPS, Amazon, and FedEx drivers do the same. But not so with the trash trucks, which will leave you and your trash disappointed for that week if easy access is not available.

The gentleman who lived across the street from our estate sale in Garland had already shaken his fist at me the first morning of the sale. At the break of dawn, cars lined his side of the street, precluding any access to his mailbox. I quietly gave him the speech I had given dozens of times before to neighbors: "Sorry, sir, actually you're mistaken, you don't really own the patch of street in front of your house. The city owns the roads." (More fist-shaking, and a "Gol' darn it.")

For the next three days, this gentleman sat in a folding chair in the street in front of his mailbox. Seven hours a day, or until the mail came each day. At lunchtime, his wife brought him a food tray. I could see him through the window by my checkout desk, and sometimes I heard him yell at drivers who got too close to his folding throne in front of the mailbox. The day it started raining, he sat with an umbrella. He didn't have a book or a newspaper. But he had commitment!!
