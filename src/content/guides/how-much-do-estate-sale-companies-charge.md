I ran Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales in the Dallas/Fort Worth area for over twenty years, so let me give you the straight answer that most estate sale companies dance around on their websites: we charged a commission on total sales, and the industry standard is 25% to 50%.

That's a big range, and I know it raises more questions than it answers. So let me break down exactly what that percentage means, what affects it, what you should be getting for your money, and why hiring a professional almost always nets you more than doing it yourself — even after the commission.

## The Industry Standard: 25% to 50%

Most estate sale companies work on a commission-only model. That means:

- The company takes a percentage of total sales
- The client (you) receives the remaining percentage
- There's typically no upfront fee

So if a sale generates $10,000 in revenue and the commission rate is 35%, the company keeps $3,500 and you receive $6,500.

Some companies charge at the lower end (25-30%), some at the higher end (40-50%), and most fall somewhere in the middle around 35%. The rate isn't random — several factors determine where on that spectrum a particular sale lands.

## What Determines the Commission Rate

### The Value of the Estate

This is the biggest factor. A home filled with high-quality furniture, art, antiques, and collectibles — where the expected sale total is $30,000 or more — will typically get a lower commission rate, often 25-30%. The dollar amount the company earns is still substantial, even at the lower percentage.

A more modest home with everyday furniture and household goods — where the expected total might be $5,000-10,000 — usually comes with a higher commission rate, often 35-40%. The company is doing roughly the same amount of work (sorting, pricing, staging, advertising, staffing the sale, cleanup), but the revenue pool is smaller.

I always tried to be upfront about this math. When someone called about a house that was clearly on the lower-value end, I'd explain: "The work involved is about the same regardless of what's in the house. It takes just as long to sort and price a kitchen full of basic cookware as it does a kitchen full of Le Creuset."

### The Size and Condition of the Home

A clean, well-organized 1,800-square-foot home is a very different job than a 4,000-square-foot home that hasn't been decluttered in thirty years. More square footage, more rooms, more stuff — that means more labor hours, more team members, and more days of setup.

I once walked into a home for a consultation and the homeowner cheerfully warned me, "We're a little bit of packrats." This was like saying the ocean is "a little bit wet." Every room was filled. The garage was impassable. There was a storage unit. And a barn. That sale required a higher commission because the prep work alone took eight days.

### Location

Geography matters. If the company has to travel a significant distance from their home base, the commission might be higher to account for the additional time and expense. Most companies have a service area, and jobs outside that radius come with adjusted terms.

### Special Circumstances

Some sales require extra services: professional appraisals, specialty marketing for high-end items, coordination with realtors or attorneys, rush timelines, or post-sale cleanout. These can affect the commission rate or be billed separately.

## What You Should Get for That Commission

A good estate sale company earns their commission. Here's what should be included:

### Pre-Sale Work

- **Initial walkthrough and consultation** — assessing the home, estimating potential revenue, discussing the process
- **Sorting and organizing** — going through every room, closet, cabinet, and drawer
- **Research and pricing** — determining fair market value for every sellable item, bringing in specialists for high-value pieces
- **Staging** — arranging the home into a professional shopping environment
- **Photography** — taking quality photos for online listings and advertising

### Advertising and Marketing

- **Listing on estate sale directories** — EstateSales.net, EstateSales.org, and any regional platforms
- **Email marketing** — notifying their existing buyer database (a good company will have thousands of subscribers)
- **Social media promotion** — posts on Facebook, Instagram, and relevant groups
- **Signage** — professional directional signs in the neighborhood and surrounding area

### Sale Days

- **Staffing** — trained team members to manage crowds, answer questions, prevent theft, and process sales
- **Cash and credit card processing** — handling all transactions
- **Crowd management** — numbered ticket systems, controlled entry, security

### Post-Sale

- **Unsold item management** — coordinating donations, cleanout, or consignment
- **Accounting** — detailed sales reporting
- **Payment** — a check to the client, usually within two weeks of the sale

If a company is quoting you a high commission but only providing a fraction of these services, that's a red flag. Ask specifically what's included.

## Red Flags in Contracts

After two decades in this industry, I've heard the horror stories. Here's what to watch out for:

### Upfront Fees

Most reputable estate sale companies do not charge an upfront fee. They make money when you make money — that's the whole point of the commission model. If a company wants $500 or $1,000 upfront before they do any work, ask why. There are *occasional* legitimate reasons (extremely remote locations, very small estates that won't generate much revenue), but in general, upfront fees are a yellow flag.

### Vague Commission Structures

The contract should clearly state the commission percentage, how total sales are calculated, and when you'll be paid. If it's vague or confusing, ask for clarification in writing. "We'll work all that out after the sale" is not an acceptable answer.

### No Insurance

Any professional estate sale company should carry liability insurance. They're bringing the public into a private home. If someone trips on a rug and breaks their wrist, you want to know the company's insurance covers it — not yours. Ask for proof of insurance before signing anything.

### No References

A company that's been in business for any length of time should be happy to provide references from recent clients. If they can't or won't, move on.

### Unrealistic Promises

If a company promises your sale will generate a specific dollar amount before they've even done a thorough walkthrough, be skeptical. Experienced companies can give you a rough range based on what they see, but guarantees? No honest company makes guarantees. There are too many variables — weather, market conditions, buyer turnout — that affect results.

### Items Disappearing

This is the nightmare scenario, and unfortunately, it does happen with disreputable operators. A legitimate company should provide a detailed inventory of high-value items before the sale. After the sale, you should receive a report showing what sold, for how much, and what remains. If items vanish without explanation, you've got a serious problem.

## Questions to Ask Before You Hire

When you're interviewing estate sale companies (and you should interview at least three), here are the questions that matter:

1. **What is your commission rate, and what does it include?**
2. **How many sales have you conducted?**
3. **Can you provide three references from recent clients?**
4. **Do you carry liability insurance?**
5. **How do you handle pricing — do you research items individually or use flat pricing?**
6. **How large is your buyer mailing list?**
7. **Where do you advertise?**
8. **How many staff members will be at the sale?**
9. **What happens to unsold items?**
10. **When will I receive payment after the sale?**

The answers will tell you a lot about whether this company is professional, transparent, and worth their commission.

## The DIY Alternative (And Why It Usually Doesn't Work)

I understand the temptation. You look at a 35% commission and think: "I could just do this myself and keep all the money." And technically, you can. Nobody's stopping you from pricing everything in your parents' house and holding a sale.

Here's why it almost never works out the way people hope:

### You Don't Have the Buyer Network

When I sent out an email blast about an upcoming sale, it went to over 8,000 local subscribers who had specifically signed up to hear about estate sales in the DFW area. Plus our social media following, plus our listings on the major estate sale directories. That kind of reach takes years to build. Your Craigslist post and neighborhood Facebook group aren't going to match it.

### You'll Misprice Things

I don't say this to be condescending — I say it because I mispriced things regularly during my first few years, and I was doing this full-time. Pricing estate sale items accurately requires broad knowledge of antiques, collectibles, furniture markets, brand values, and current trends. Without that knowledge, you will overprice some things (they won't sell) and underprice others (you'll lose money). The net result is almost always less total revenue than a professional would generate.

### It's Emotionally Brutal

Running a sale of your own family's belongings is a completely different experience than running a sale as a detached professional. When a stranger picks up your mother's favorite teacup, inspects it, puts it back down, and says "eh" — that hurts in a way you don't expect. When someone offers you $2 for something your father treasured, you'll either accept it and feel sick or refuse it and lose the sale. Emotional detachment is a *feature* of hiring a professional, not just a luxury.

### The Math Usually Favors the Pro

Let's say a DIY sale generates $5,000 because you didn't have the reach, the pricing knowledge, or the staging skills of a professional. You kept 100% — great, you made $5,000.

Now let's say a professional company runs the same sale and generates $12,000 because they had a huge buyer list, priced everything accurately, staged the home beautifully, and ran a tight three-day operation. At 35% commission, they keep $4,200 and you get $7,800.

You hired a pro, paid their commission, and *still took home $2,800 more* than you would have on your own. This isn't hypothetical — I saw this scenario play out over and over throughout my career.

## The Bottom Line

Estate sale companies charge what they charge because the work is real, the expertise is genuine, and the results speak for themselves. A good company will be transparent about their fees, clear about what's included, and honest about what your estate is likely to generate.

Is 35% a lot of money? On a $20,000 sale, that's $7,000. I won't pretend that's nothing. But for that $7,000, you're getting a team of professionals who will spend a week turning your home into a retail operation, bring in hundreds of serious buyers, price everything to maximize your return, and handle every detail from setup to cleanup.

After twenty years in this business, I firmly believe the commission model works because everyone's incentives are aligned. We made more money when our clients made more money. That's the way it should be.
