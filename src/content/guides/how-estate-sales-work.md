People think running an estate sale is just sticking price tags on stuff and opening the front door. I thought so too, back when I started Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales in the Dallas/Fort Worth area over twenty years ago. It took about three sales to realize I had no idea what I was getting into — and about a hundred more to get genuinely good at it.

Here's how the process actually works, from first phone call to final check, told through the stories of what really happens behind the scenes.

## Phase 1: The Initial Phone Call

It usually starts with a phone call. Someone's mother just passed away and they're standing in her house, overwhelmed. Or a couple is downsizing from their forever home and they don't know where to begin. Or a realtor needs a house emptied before it goes on the market.

The first thing I always asked was: "What's the situation?" Not "what do you have?" — the situation. Because understanding *why* someone is having an estate sale tells you everything about how to handle it. A family dealing with grief needs a different approach than a couple excited about their move to a condo on the beach.

I remember one call from a man whose wife had just been moved to memory care. He was sitting in their living room, surrounded by fifty years of married life, and he said, "I don't even know where to start." I told him, "You don't have to. That's what we do." I could hear him exhale through the phone. That exhale — that relief — is why this job matters.

## Phase 2: The Walkthrough

The walkthrough is where the real work begins. I'd schedule a time to visit the home, walk through every single room, and assess what we were working with.

This is part evaluation, part detective work. You're looking at the big picture — how many rooms, how much furniture, what's the overall quality level — but you're also looking for the hidden stuff. The things people don't think to mention because they don't know they're valuable.

During a walkthrough in Colleyville, the homeowner casually pointed to a stack of old magazines in the garage and said, "Oh, and there's some junk out here too." That "junk" included a complete set of Life magazines from the 1960s, a box of vintage fishing lures worth over $2,000, and a first-edition copy of a Cormac McCarthy novel tucked between some Reader's Digests.

I learned to look everywhere. Under beds. Behind water heaters. Inside pockets of coats in the back of closets. In the freezer. (People hide things in freezers more often than you'd think.) One time I found a diamond ring wrapped in a paper towel in a butter dish. The family had been looking for it for months.

After the walkthrough, I'd give the client an honest assessment: what we could likely sell, a rough estimate of expected revenue, our commission structure, and a realistic timeline. Then we'd sign a contract and get to work.

## Phase 3: Sorting and Organizing

This is the phase nobody sees and everybody underestimates. Before we can price a single item, we have to go through every inch of the house and organize everything into sellable categories.

We'd start with one room at a time. Pull everything out. Sort it. Group similar items together. Set aside anything the family wants to keep. Flag anything that needs special research or appraisal. Throw away actual trash (and yes, there's always actual trash — expired medications, broken items, things that are genuinely unsellable).

I remember a house in Southlake where the homeowner had been a serious collector. Of what, you ask? Everything. Coins. Stamps. Vintage toys. Political memorabilia. Cuckoo clocks. He had a spare bedroom that was floor-to-ceiling boxes, and it took my team two full days just to sort that room. We found things in there that hadn't seen daylight since the Reagan administration.

Sorting is also when you find the personal items that stop you in your tracks. Love letters. Journals. Military medals. Baby shoes from 1955. Photo albums documenting an entire family's history. We always set those aside for the family, even if they told us to sell everything. Some things shouldn't have a price tag.

## Phase 4: Research and Pricing

Once everything is sorted and organized, we start pricing. This is where experience and knowledge earn their keep.

For everyday household items — kitchenware, linens, basic furniture, books, clothing — we relied on our extensive experience and knowledge of what the market will bear. After running hundreds of sales, you develop an internal pricing database. You know a good cast-iron skillet sells for $15-25. You know vintage Pyrex has a cult following and that certain patterns command serious money. You know that a brand-name stand mixer in good condition will sell within the first hour.

For higher-value items — antiques, fine art, jewelry, rare collectibles, high-end furniture — we'd do targeted research. Check auction records. Review completed sales on eBay and other platforms. Consult reference books. And when something was truly out of our wheelhouse, we'd bring in a specialist.

One pricing story I'll never forget: a family told us the oil painting in their father's den was "just something he picked up at a flea market." Something about it caught my eye — the brushwork, the framing, the way it was hung in a place of honor despite the offhand story. We had an art appraiser take a look. Turned out it was worth considerably more than "flea market" money. I called the family and told them, and the daughter said, "I think Dad knew exactly what he had. He just liked telling people it was from a flea market."

The biggest pricing mistake amateurs make is pricing based on what they *paid* for something rather than what it's *worth now*. That oak entertainment center cost $2,500 in 1997. Today? You'll be lucky to get $75. The market doesn't care about your receipts.

## Phase 5: Staging

Staging is what separates a professional estate sale from a house full of stuff with price stickers on it. We're not just putting things out — we're creating a shopping environment.

Furniture gets arranged to show it off, not crammed against walls. Dining tables get set with china and flatware so buyers can picture the whole setup in their own home. Closets get organized by type and color. Tools get laid out on the workbench so buyers can see what's available at a glance. Art gets hung at eye level with good lighting.

We'd also create a logical flow through the house. You don't want fifty people bottlenecked in a narrow hallway. Strategic placement of high-interest items in different parts of the house keeps people moving.

I'll confess something: I always staged the kitchen last because I liked doing it the most. There's something deeply satisfying about organizing someone's kitchen into a beautiful display. All the bakeware together, the vintage mixing bowls arranged by size, the spice rack cleaned up and shining. A well-staged kitchen is the heart of a good estate sale.

The night before the sale, we'd do our final walkthrough. Check every price tag. Make sure nothing got missed. Test the credit card reader. Put fresh batteries in the calculator. Lay out bags and wrapping materials. Set up the cashier station near the front door.

Then you go home, try to sleep, and show up at 6 AM ready for whatever the day throws at you.

## Phase 6: Sale Days

A typical estate sale runs Thursday through Saturday, though some companies do Friday through Sunday. Here's how those days usually break down.

### Thursday (Day One): The Serious Buyers

Thursday is when the dealers, collectors, and seasoned estate sale shoppers show up. These people know what they're doing. They've studied the photos online. They have a mental list of what they want. Some of them have been hitting estate sales every week for years.

We'd open doors at 9 AM, but people would start lining up as early as 7. We used a numbered ticket system — show up, get a number, and we'd call numbers in order to manage the flow inside the house. Depending on the size of the home, we'd let ten to twenty people in at a time.

The first hour is the most intense. Buyers moving fast, grabbing items, asking questions. "Is this real silver?" "What's the maker on this table?" "Do you have any more of these in the back?" (There is no "back." This is someone's house.)

My team would be stationed throughout — someone in each major room to answer questions, someone at the door managing entry, and someone at the cashier station. We stayed busy from open to close.

### Friday (Day Two): The Deals Start

Most companies offer a discount on day two — typically 25% off everything. This brings in a different crowd: bargain hunters who don't mind missing the cream of the crop in exchange for lower prices. The furniture that didn't sell on Thursday at full price starts moving now.

Fridays had a more relaxed energy. The frantic first-day rush was over, and people browsed more slowly. This was often when I'd have the best conversations with buyers. They'd tell me about their collections, ask about the previous owner, share stories about similar items they'd found at other sales. The estate sale community is a genuine community, and Fridays felt like its living room.

### Saturday (Day Three): Everything Must Go

Saturday is clearance day. Half off, or sometimes more. This is when the house really starts emptying out. People buy things on Saturday they'd never buy at full price — entire sets of dishes for ten dollars, boxes of books for a buck, furniture at a fraction of its value.

We'd also get the "what will you take for all of it?" offers. Someone would point at everything remaining in the garage and offer a lump sum. If the number was reasonable and it meant we didn't have to deal with the leftovers, we'd often say yes.

By Saturday afternoon, the house that was bursting with a lifetime of belongings three days ago would be mostly empty. A few things on the walls, some items too heavy or too niche to sell. It was always a little surreal seeing it like that — this home that had been so full of someone's life, now echoing and bare.

## Phase 7: Post-Sale Cleanup

After the last buyer leaves, we pack up the unsold items. The family decides what happens next — donate to charity, send to a consignment shop, or arrange for a cleanout service to haul everything away.

We'd do a final sweep of the house to make sure we hadn't missed anything. More than once we found stray items in odd places — a necklace that slipped behind a dresser, a price tag that fell off a painting, cash that dropped out of someone's pocket. (We always tracked down the buyer if we could.)

Then comes the paperwork. We'd tally up total sales, calculate the commission split, and prepare a detailed report for the client. Within a week or two of the sale, the client would receive their check.

## Phase 8: The Settlement

The final step is sitting down with the client (or talking by phone) to go over the results. How much was sold, what the commission breakdown looked like, and what happened with any remaining items.

I always tried to share a few highlights — the item that got the most attention, the thing that sold for way more than expected, the funny moment from the sale. It helped put a positive cap on what was often an emotional process.

One of my favorite settlement conversations was with a woman in her eighties who'd downsized from her family home to an apartment. She'd been worried nobody would want her things. I got to hand her a check and tell her that her house had been one of our best sales of the year. That people had lined up to buy her furniture, her art, her kitchen supplies. That her things were going to new homes where they'd be loved and used. She teared up, and honestly, so did I.

## How to Have Your Own Estate Sale

If you're reading this because you're thinking about having an estate sale, here's my honest advice:

**Hire a professional.** I know I'm biased, but after twenty-plus years, I've seen too many people try to do it themselves and leave thousands of dollars on the table. A good estate sale company has the experience to price correctly, the customer base to draw buyers, and the systems to run a smooth operation.

**Interview at least three companies.** Ask about their commission rates, their advertising methods, their insurance, and their references. A reputable company will answer every question openly.

**Don't clean the house first.** I know this sounds counterintuitive, but we've had clients throw away things that were actually valuable because they didn't know what they had. Let the professionals go through everything first.

**Take what you want before we start.** Once we begin setup, the house becomes our workspace. Decide in advance what the family is keeping and remove it before we arrive.

**Be patient with the process.** A good estate sale takes a week or more to prepare. Rushing it means missed items, bad pricing, and lower turnout.

Estate sales are emotional, chaotic, exhausting, and surprisingly meaningful. Every single one is different. And after doing this for over twenty years, I can honestly say: there is no such thing as a boring estate sale.
