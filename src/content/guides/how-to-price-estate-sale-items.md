If there's one thing that makes or breaks an estate sale, it's the pricing. Price too high and everything sits there while buyers walk out empty-handed. Price too low and you've just given away the client's inheritance. After running estate sales for over twenty years in the Dallas/Fort Worth area, I can tell you: pricing is the hardest part of this job, and it never stops being humbling.

Here's what I've learned about pricing — the strategies that work, the mistakes that cost people real money, and a few stories that still keep me up at night.

## The Golden Rule: It's Worth What Someone Will Pay

Before we get into categories, let me share the single most important pricing lesson I ever learned. It came during my first year running Lucy & Ethel Estate Sales, when a client pointed to a painting in her living room and said, "My husband paid $3,000 for that in 1985."

I priced it at $1,500, figuring I was being generous with the discount. It sat there for three days. Nobody even asked about it. After the sale, I did more research and realized the artist had faded into obscurity. The painting was worth maybe $75 to the right buyer.

What someone paid for something is irrelevant. What it sold for at another estate sale last month is irrelevant. The only question that matters is: what will a real person hand you real cash for, today, in this market?

That mindset shift changed everything for me.

## Pricing by Category

### Furniture

Furniture is where the biggest money is — and where the biggest pricing mistakes happen. Here's the general framework:

**High-demand styles:** Mid-century modern (think Danish teak, Eames, Herman Miller) commands premium prices and has for years. Quality traditional pieces — Henredon, Drexel Heritage, Ethan Allen — still sell well. Industrial and farmhouse styles have strong followings.

**Low-demand styles:** That Mediterranean-style bedroom set from the late '80s? The ornate dark-wood dining suite that seats twelve? The massive oak entertainment center built for a tube TV? The market for these has collapsed. I've watched gorgeous, well-made furniture sell for almost nothing because it doesn't fit modern tastes or modern homes.

**My rule of thumb:** Price good furniture at 20-30% of its original retail value, adjusted for condition and current demand. For designer or mid-century pieces, research comparable sales — you might be surprised how high (or low) the market has moved.

**The story:** We had a teak credenza at a sale in University Park that I priced at $450 based on recent comparable sales. A dealer bought it in the first ten minutes. I later saw it listed in his shop for $1,800. Did I underprice it? Maybe. But it sold immediately, the client was happy with the return, and trying to get retail prices at an estate sale is a recipe for unsold inventory. You're not running an antique store — you're running a three-day event.

### Kitchenware

Kitchens are surprisingly profitable if you know what you're looking at.

**The heavy hitters:** Le Creuset, Staub, All-Clad, KitchenAid stand mixers, quality knife sets (Wusthof, Henckels), cast-iron skillets (especially vintage Lodge, Griswold, or Wagner). These items have loyal followings and hold value remarkably well. A well-used Le Creuset Dutch oven can sell for 40-50% of retail.

**The collectibles:** Vintage Pyrex is a phenomenon. Certain patterns — Butterprint, Gooseberry, Lucky in Love — have fanatic collectors who will pay serious money. I once priced a Pyrex bowl at $5 because I didn't know better. A collector bought it and very kindly told me it was worth about $80. That was an expensive lesson I only needed to learn once.

**The everyday stuff:** Regular pots, pans, utensils, and gadgets move fast at $1-5 each. Don't overthink it. Bundle common items into lots when possible — "entire drawer of utensils, $3."

### Clothing

Clothing at estate sales is a volume game, with occasional gems.

**Everyday clothing:** $2-5 per item, grouped by type on racks or tables. Don't try to price every individual t-shirt and pair of pants — the time isn't worth it.

**Designer and luxury brands:** These get individual attention. A Chanel jacket, a Burberry trench, Ferragamo shoes — research the brand, check the condition, and price accordingly. Vintage designer clothing, especially from the '60s through '80s, can be worth significantly more than modern pieces.

**Vintage:** Anything genuinely vintage (generally pre-1990) with cool styling, interesting labels, or cultural cachet gets priced higher. Western wear, Hawaiian shirts, military jackets, band tees — these all have collector markets.

**The story:** We found a closet in a Flower Mound home that was basically a time capsule. The woman had kept every piece of clothing she'd worn since the 1960s, all in beautiful condition, organized by decade. We brought in a vintage clothing specialist for that one. The closet alone generated more revenue than the rest of the house combined.

### Jewelry

Jewelry is simultaneously the most valuable and the most nerve-wracking category to price.

**Fine jewelry:** Gold, silver, diamonds, gemstones — these need proper evaluation. At minimum, test metals with a magnet and acid test kit. Weigh gold and silver pieces and know the current spot prices. For significant pieces, bring in a certified appraiser. Never guess on jewelry.

**Costume jewelry:** Don't dismiss it. Signed pieces by designers like Miriam Haskell, Trifari, Coro, and Weiss can be worth hundreds. Even unsigned vintage costume jewelry has a strong collector market. I'd sort costume jewelry into two tiers: interesting pieces displayed individually with researched prices, and everyday pieces bundled into grab bags for $5-10.

**The cautionary tale:** Early in my career, I priced a "costume" brooch at $8 because it looked like something you'd find at a department store. A jeweler came in, looked at it for about three seconds, and bought it. It was a signed piece worth considerably more. After that, every single piece of jewelry that came through our sales got a proper look. Every single one.

### Books

Books are the most labor-intensive, lowest-margin category at most estate sales — with spectacular exceptions.

**The reality:** Most books sell for $1-3 each. Paperbacks, book club editions, bestsellers from ten years ago — they're hard to give away in the age of e-readers and minimalism.

**The exceptions:** First editions, signed copies, rare printings, and books on niche topics with dedicated collector bases. A first-edition *To Kill a Mockingbird* in decent condition is worth thousands. Even less famous first editions can surprise you. Always check the copyright page, look for first-edition indicators, and search completed sales for anything that looks remotely old or unusual.

**My approach:** Spend your research time on books published before 1970, anything with a signature, anything that looks hand-bound or unusual, and collections focused on specific topics (local history, rare hobbies, academic specialties). Everything else gets the $1-2 treatment.

### Electronics

Here's the short version: electronics depreciate faster than anything else in the house.

**Current-generation items** (less than two years old, working condition): Price at 30-50% of retail. People will buy a working iPad, a good Bluetooth speaker, or a recent gaming console at a steep discount.

**Older electronics:** Almost worthless with very few exceptions. That five-year-old TV? $25, maybe. The Bose sound system from 2008? $30 if it works. The VCR and the CD tower? A dollar each and say a prayer.

**Vintage electronics:** Turntables, reel-to-reel players, vintage receivers and speakers from brands like Marantz, McIntosh, and Pioneer have a passionate market. These can be the electronics equivalent of finding that mid-century credenza. Research before pricing.

### Collectibles

This is where estate sales get really interesting — and really tricky.

Collectibles are only worth what current collectors will pay, and collector markets shift constantly. Beanie Babies that were "worth" thousands in the '90s are basically worthless now. Meanwhile, vintage Star Wars figures, certain Lladro pieces, Hummel figurines (select ones), and specific pottery lines have held or increased in value.

**My advice:** Never assume a collection is valuable just because someone spent decades building it. And never assume it's worthless. Research the specific items, check current market values, and price accordingly. If it's a truly specialized collection — rare coins, stamps, antique firearms, fine art — bring in an expert. Their fee will pay for itself.

## The Five Biggest Pricing Mistakes

### 1. Emotional Pricing

"But Mom loved that lamp." I understand. I really do. But the buyer doesn't know your mom, and the lamp is worth $12. The hardest part of my job was gently explaining to families that sentimental value and market value are completely different things. If an item is too emotionally loaded to sell at market price, don't sell it. Keep it. But don't put it in the sale at an inflated price because of what it means to you.

### 2. Retail Fantasy

Pricing estate sale items at or near their original retail value virtually guarantees they won't sell. People come to estate sales for deals. If your prices match what they'd pay at a furniture store — where they'd get something new with a warranty — they'll walk right past your stuff.

### 3. Ignoring Current Trends

The antique market changes constantly. Brown furniture (dark wood traditional pieces) has been in a slump for years. Mid-century modern has been hot but is showing signs of leveling off. Certain collectibles that were worthless five years ago are now in demand. You have to know what's selling *now*, not what was selling when you started in the business.

### 4. The Herd Mentality

Just because another estate sale company priced a similar item at $500 doesn't mean yours is worth $500. Condition matters. Location matters. The specific buyer pool that day matters. Price based on your own research and experience, not on what someone else did.

### 5. Not Researching the Outliers

Most items in a house are straightforward to price. But every house has a few outliers — the weird thing in the basement, the item nobody recognizes, the collection in the spare room. These are where the biggest wins and biggest losses happen. Research them. Every time. The twenty minutes you spend Googling a strange figurine could be the difference between pricing it at $5 and pricing it at $500.

## The Art Behind the Science

After all the research and frameworks and market analysis, pricing still comes down to judgment. You develop an instinct over time — a feel for what the market will bear at this particular sale, in this particular neighborhood, for this particular mix of items.

Some days you nail it and everything sells by noon on Thursday. Some days you're second-guessing yourself by Friday afternoon as buyers walk past items you were sure would fly. That's the humbling part. After a thousand sales, I still had days where the market surprised me.

But here's what I tell anyone who asks: when in doubt, price to sell. An item that sells at a fair price is better than an item that sits there unsold. Your job isn't to get the maximum possible dollar for every single thing — it's to move the entire household efficiently and generate the best overall return for your client.

That philosophy served me well for twenty years. I hope it serves you too.
